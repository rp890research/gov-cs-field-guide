# gov-cs-field-guide

A practical field guide to Customer Success in UK government and national security accounts. Written from direct experience across the Cabinet Office, National Crime Agency, Ministry of Defence, and wider Whitehall.

Most CS playbooks are written for commercial SaaS customers. Government accounts operate differently: procurement cycles are longer, stakeholder maps are more complex, budget signals are harder to read, and the decision-making culture is unlike anything in the private sector. This guide covers the gaps.

## Contents

### 1. Understanding the procurement cycle
Government customers do not renew on a commercial timeline. Budget cycles run April to March. Procurement decisions often require internal business cases, approval chains, and in some cases formal tender processes even for renewals. Plan your renewal motion 6 months out minimum, not 90 days.

### 2. Stakeholder mapping in Whitehall
Government accounts have more stakeholders than they appear to. The day-to-day user is rarely the budget holder. The budget holder is rarely the strategic sponsor. Map all three layers early: users, commercial leads, and senior responsible owners. Losing access to any one layer is a churn signal.

### 3. Building trust with senior civil servants and intelligence community customers
Senior civil servants and intelligence community professionals are expert, sceptical, and time-poor. They do not respond to sales-led engagement. The fastest way to build trust is to demonstrate deep understanding of their operational context before you ask for anything. Lead with their problem, not your product.

### 4. Reading budget signals
In government accounts, budget pressure rarely surfaces as a direct conversation. It appears as delayed responses, reduced meeting frequency, procurement queries about contract structure, and questions about value evidence. Treat any of these as amber signals and engage proactively.

### 5. The renewal conversation in government
Government renewals are not won in the renewal conversation. They are won in the 12 months before it. Consistent value delivery, documented outcomes, and executive relationships built at pace are what make renewal straightforward. If you are having a difficult renewal conversation, the problem started much earlier.

### 6. Expansion in government accounts
Government expansion often requires a different commercial mechanism than commercial SaaS. Additional licences, new departments, and new use cases may each require separate procurement processes. Identify expansion opportunities early and help the customer navigate their own internal process. The NCA growth from 770k to 1.2M was driven by attending the National Financial Investigators Conference, supporting live case reviews, and understanding the customer's internal funding structure well enough to help them make the case internally.

### 7. Executive engagement in defence and national security
Defence and national security accounts operate on relationships as much as contracts. Formal QBRs matter less than consistent senior-level access. Invest in understanding the organisational culture, the chain of command, and the informal influence networks. Ceremonial moments (covenant signings, industry events, senior briefings) carry real weight in this environment and should be used deliberately.

### 8. Metrics that resonate in government
Government customers are sceptical of vendor-defined metrics. Frame value in terms of their own outcomes: investigations closed, time saved per analyst, cost per case, reduction in manual processing. Avoid platform-centric metrics like logins and feature adoption as the primary value story.

## Who this is for

CS leaders and CSMs working with UK central government, law enforcement, intelligence community, or defence customers. Also relevant for anyone selling into regulated, mission-critical environments where the commercial playbook does not fully apply.
